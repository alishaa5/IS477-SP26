## Summary

Higher education is one of the largest financial investments that individuals make in their lifetimes, yet many students make enrollment decisions with limited information about whether higher tuition actually translates to better financial outcomes after graduation. The cost of attending college has risen significantly over the past few decades, outpacing inflation and wage growth in most sectors. According to federal data, the average student loan borrower now graduates with tens of thousands of dollars in debt, and many do not see earnings that justify those costs within the first decade after leaving school. Student loan debt has become a defining financial challenge for millions of Americans, influencing major life decisions such as whether to buy a home, start a family, or pursue further education. Despite the scale of this issue, there is limited publicly accessible analysis that directly and systematically connects what students pay to what they actually earn afterward, broken down in a way that is easy to understand and act on.

This project was motivated by the belief that data-driven analysis of the relationship between tuition, debt, and post-graduation earnings can help students, families, and policymakers make more informed decisions about higher education. By using federal datasets that cover thousands of institutions across the United States, we aimed to produce an analysis that goes beyond anecdote and captures the full landscape of higher education costs and outcomes.

This project investigates the relationship between college tuition costs and post-graduation career outcomes for institutions across the United States. The central research questions guiding this work are whether there is a meaningful relationship between tuition cost and median earnings after graduation, how student debt levels relate to post-graduate earnings at the institution level, whether certain types of institutions provide stronger financial outcomes relative to their tuition costs, and how institutional characteristics such as public versus private classification and institution level influence the financial outcomes of graduates. These questions are directly relevant to students deciding where to enroll, to policymakers considering how to regulate tuition or student lending, and to researchers studying the economics of higher education.

To answer these questions, we integrated three complementary federal datasets: the U.S. Department of Education College Scorecard, the IPEDS Institutional Characteristics file, and the IPEDS Student Financial Aid file. All three datasets were linked using the shared UNITID identifier that uniquely identifies each postsecondary institution in federal education data systems. The College Scorecard provided the core financial variables including tuition, median debt, and median earnings. The IPEDS datasets provided institutional context including control type, geographic location, and financial aid distribution. Together, these three sources allowed us to construct a comprehensive institution-level dataset covering costs, debt, earnings, and institutional characteristics for thousands of colleges and universities.

Our workflow proceeds through five automated stages: data acquisition via acquire.py, data cleaning via clean.py, dataset integration via merge.py, analysis and visualization via analyze.py, and end-to-end orchestration via a Snakemake workflow. Each stage produces documented outputs and can be reproduced independently or as part of the full pipeline. The workflow is designed so that anyone with Python and an internet connection can reproduce our results from scratch.

Key findings from the analysis indicate that private nonprofit institutions tend to produce graduates with higher median earnings ten years after enrollment compared to public or for-profit institutions. The data also shows that the majority of institutions fall above the line where earnings equal debt, meaning that graduates at most schools earn more annually than their total accumulated debt by the ten-year mark. However, a meaningful cluster of private for-profit institutions falls at or below this line, indicating an unfavorable debt-to-earnings ratio for their graduates. Tuition at four-year institutions is substantially higher and more variable than at two-year institutions. These patterns collectively suggest that institution type and level are more reliable predictors of financial outcomes than tuition cost alone, and that high tuition does not guarantee high earnings.

Overall, this project demonstrates how publicly available federal education data can be integrated and analyzed to produce actionable insights about the financial value of higher education. The findings are not definitive because selection effects, field of study, and individual circumstances all play a significant role in shaping graduate outcomes. However, they provide a useful data-driven baseline for understanding how the financial outcomes of higher education vary across the landscape of American colleges and universities. The complete workflow, from raw data download to final visualizations, is automated and reproducible by anyone with a Python environment and internet access.

## Data Profile

### Dataset 1: College Scorecard

Source: https://collegescorecard.ed.gov/data/

The College Scorecard is maintained by the U.S. Department of Education and is the primary federal resource for comparing college costs and student outcomes at the institution level. The Scorecard was launched publicly in 2015 as part of a federal initiative to improve transparency in higher education and help students make more informed enrollment decisions. It draws on administrative data from multiple federal sources including the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA), federal tax records, and institutional reporting to IPEDS. Because it combines data from multiple administrative systems, it provides a uniquely rich picture of the relationship between institutional characteristics, student finances, and post-graduation earnings.

The version used in this project is the most recent institution-level cohort file, released in March 2026. After being downloaded as a ZIP archive by acquire.py, the extracted CSV is stored in data/raw/. The file is wide-format with one row per institution and several thousand columns covering a broad range of institutional and student outcome variables. For this project, we selected a focused subset of variables most directly relevant to our research questions.

UNITID serves as the unique institution identifier and is the primary key used to join this dataset with the two IPEDS datasets. INSTNM provides the full institution name. STABBR provides the two-letter state abbreviation indicating where the institution is located. CONTROL is a coded variable indicating whether the institution is public (1), private nonprofit (2), or private for-profit (3). ICLEVEL indicates the level of the institution, coded as 4-year (1), 2-year (2), or less-than-2-year (3). TUITIONFEE_IN and TUITIONFEE_OUT record the published in-state and out-of-state tuition and fees respectively for the most recent academic year available. DEBT_MDN records the median cumulative loan debt at the time of graduation or program completion for students who completed a program and received federal aid. MD_EARN_WNE_P10 records the median earnings of students who were working and not enrolled ten years after initial enrollment, derived from federal tax records matched to student aid records.

This dataset is publicly available from a U.S. federal government agency and is released under an open data policy. It contains no personally identifiable information. All reported values are institution-level aggregates computed from student-level administrative records. The Department of Education applies a privacy suppression rule: any cell whose underlying student count falls below a minimum threshold is replaced with the string "PrivacySuppressed" rather than the numeric value. This suppression protects the privacy of students at small institutions or in small subgroups but creates a significant data quality challenge, as discussed in the Data Quality section. There are no license restrictions on use or redistribution of this data beyond proper citation.

This dataset is central to all four research questions because it directly contains both the cost-side variables (tuition and debt) and the outcome-side variable (earnings), allowing us to compute relationships and ratios between them at the institution level. It is the anchor dataset around which the other two are integrated.

### Dataset 2: IPEDS Institutional Characteristics (HD2023)

Source: https://nces.ed.gov/ipeds/datacenter/data/HD2023.zip

The Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS) is the primary federal data collection for postsecondary education in the United States and is administered by the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES), which is part of the U.S. Department of Education. IPEDS collects data from all institutions that participate in federal student financial aid programs, which means it covers the vast majority of degree-granting postsecondary institutions in the country. The system is organized into a series of survey components, each covering a different aspect of institutional operations, and data is collected annually.

The Institutional Characteristics survey component, referred to as HD2023, covers the 2023 survey cycle and provides directory and classification information for all Title IV eligible institutions. It is the authoritative federal source for institutional identifiers and basic descriptive characteristics. After being downloaded as a ZIP archive and extracted by acquire.py, the CSV is stored in data/raw/. The file contains one row per institution and includes variables covering institution name, address, geographic location, control type, institution level, degree offerings, and accreditation status, among others.

The variables used from this dataset include UNITID as the join key, INSTNM for institution name, CITY and STABBR for geographic location, CONTROL for institution control type, and ICLEVEL for institution level. These variables largely overlap with those in the College Scorecard but serve as an authoritative cross-reference for institutional classification. In cases where classification variables differ slightly between the two sources due to differences in collection timing or methodology, the IPEDS values provide a consistent reference grounded in the official federal survey cycle.

This dataset is publicly released federal survey data from NCES. There are no restrictions on use or redistribution beyond proper citation. No personally identifiable information is included. The data relates to our research questions by providing the institutional context needed to interpret differences in tuition and earnings across different types of institutions, particularly the CONTROL and ICLEVEL variables that allow us to segment the analysis by institution type and level.

### Dataset 3: IPEDS Student Financial Aid (SFA2223)

Source: https://nces.ed.gov/ipeds/datacenter/data/SFA2223.zip

The IPEDS Student Financial Aid survey, referred to as SFA2223, covers the 2022-23 academic year and collects information about financial aid awards, net prices, and federal loan distributions at each institution. This survey component is designed to capture how institutions distribute financial aid and what students actually pay after aid is applied, providing a more nuanced view of college affordability than published tuition figures alone. After being downloaded and extracted by acquire.py, the CSV is stored in data/raw/.

The primary variables used from this dataset are UNITID for joining and FLOAN_A, which records the total dollar amount of federal loans distributed to students at each institution during the survey year. This variable was used as a proxy for whether an institution actively participates in federal financial aid programs and the scale of student borrowing at that institution. Institutions that distribute large amounts of federal loans tend to serve students who rely heavily on borrowing to finance their education, which is directly relevant to our questions about the relationship between debt and earnings.

This dataset is publicly released federal survey data from NCES and carries no use restrictions beyond proper citation. No personally identifiable information is included. The data relates to our research questions by providing a financial aid dimension that complements the tuition and debt information from the College Scorecard, allowing us to explore whether the availability and scale of federal aid at an institution is associated with different earnings outcomes for graduates. It also allows us to identify institutions where students borrow heavily, which may be at greater risk of unfavorable debt-to-earnings outcomes.

### Integration Strategy

All three datasets share the UNITID variable, which is the federal standard unique identifier for postsecondary institutions assigned by the Department of Education. This identifier is consistent across all NCES and Department of Education data products, making it a highly reliable join key that does not require any fuzzy matching or manual reconciliation. Every institution that participates in federal student aid programs has a UNITID, and this number remains stable over time unless an institution closes or undergoes a major structural change.

The College Scorecard served as the primary left-anchor table in the merge. The IPEDS Institutional Characteristics file and the IPEDS Student Financial Aid file were each joined to it using left joins on UNITID after standardizing the column to string type across all three sources. Left joins were chosen over inner joins to retain as much data from the College Scorecard as possible, even for institutions that may not appear in every IPEDS file due to differences in coverage or reporting. The final integrated dataset is saved at data/merged/merged_dataset.csv and contains one row per institution with variables drawn from all three sources.

The three datasets span slightly different collection periods. The College Scorecard uses the most recent cohort release from March 2026, the IPEDS Characteristics file uses the 2023 survey cycle, and the IPEDS Financial Aid file covers 2022-23. This temporal misalignment is a known limitation of working with administrative data from multiple sources and is discussed further in the Challenges section. Despite this limitation, UNITID matching ensures that institutions are correctly aligned across all three sources.

## Data Quality

Data quality assessment was conducted systematically across all three datasets before and after integration. The assessment covered missing values, incorrect data types, inconsistent key encoding, potential duplicate records, and distributional outliers in key financial variables. The findings of this assessment directly informed the cleaning decisions described in the Data Cleaning section.

The most pervasive data quality issue encountered was the presence of the string "PrivacySuppressed" in the College Scorecard dataset. The Department of Education applies this suppression to any numeric cell where the underlying student count is below a minimum reporting threshold, in order to protect student privacy. This suppression affected a large proportion of cells in the key financial columns including TUITIONFEE_IN, TUITIONFEE_OUT, DEBT_MDN, and MD_EARN_WNE_P10. The suppression is particularly common for small institutions, specialized programs, and subgroups within institutions. Because these strings appeared inside columns that would otherwise contain numeric values, pandas read those entire columns as object type rather than float. This meant that any arithmetic operation performed on those columns before addressing the suppressed values would either throw a TypeError or silently return NaN for entire columns without a meaningful error message. The scale of this issue was significant enough that it had to be addressed as the very first step of the cleaning process before any other operations could be performed reliably. Institutions with suppressed values in the core financial variables were effectively excluded from the analysis after the cleaning step, which reduced the size of the working dataset relative to the full Scorecard.

A second data quality issue was the inconsistent encoding of the UNITID column across the three datasets. In the College Scorecard, UNITID was stored as an integer. In the IPEDS files, it was stored as a string in some columns and an integer in others depending on how the file was exported. When datasets with mismatched UNITID types are joined in pandas using a merge operation, the join produces an empty result because integer 100654 and string "100654" are not considered equal by the equality operator used in the merge. This is a silent failure, meaning pandas does not raise an error or warning but simply returns a dataframe with no matched rows and fills all joined columns with NaN. This type of issue is particularly dangerous in data integration workflows because it can go completely undetected if the shape and content of intermediate outputs are not carefully validated after each step.

A third issue was missing data beyond the suppressed values. Even after removing the "PrivacySuppressed" strings and converting columns to numeric type, many institutions still had missing values in one or more of the key financial variables. This was particularly true for DEBT_MDN and MD_EARN_WNE_P10, which are derived from matched administrative records and may not be computable for institutions with small cohort sizes, recently opened programs, or incomplete reporting. The extent of missingness in these columns varied by institution type and size, with smaller institutions and for-profit schools showing higher rates of missing values in the earnings variables.

A fourth issue was the presence of unusual values in the tuition variables. Some institutions report in-state tuition of zero dollars, which is valid for certain public military academies or institutions that do not charge tuition in the traditional sense. Other institutions report very high tuition figures, often reflecting specialized graduate or professional schools such as medical or law schools. These values are not data entry errors and were retained in the analysis, but they affect summary statistics and distributions and are visible in the visualizations. We verified a sample of extreme values against publicly available institutional information to confirm they were plausible.

A fifth issue was minor naming inconsistencies across the IPEDS files, including differences in column name capitalization and occasional trailing whitespace in column headers. These did not affect the underlying data values but required normalization of column names before column selection operations could be applied consistently.

After integration, no duplicate UNITID values were found within any individual dataset. The merged dataset was also checked for unexpected row counts relative to the left-anchor Scorecard file, which helped detect the UNITID type mismatch issue described above.

## Data Cleaning

Data cleaning is implemented in clean.py and operates on the raw CSV files produced by acquire.py. The script is designed to be run as part of the automated Snakemake pipeline but can also be executed independently. All cleaning operations are applied programmatically and are fully reproducible. The following operations were applied in sequence, and each is directly motivated by a specific data quality issue identified during the profiling step.

The first and most critical operation was replacing all instances of the string "PrivacySuppressed" with NaN across the College Scorecard dataframe. This was done using the pandas replace method applied to the entire dataframe at once, ensuring that suppressed values in any column were uniformly converted to missing. The purpose of this operation was to convert the privacy suppression placeholders into a format that pandas recognizes as missing data, so that subsequent numeric operations would handle them correctly rather than throwing type errors or producing incorrect results. Without this step, no meaningful numeric analysis of the financial variables would be possible. This single operation is the most important cleaning step in the entire pipeline because the College Scorecard's usefulness for quantitative analysis depends entirely on being able to treat these cells as missing rather than as a string value.

The second operation was converting the key financial columns from object type to float using pd.to_numeric with the errors parameter set to coerce. The columns converted were TUITIONFEE_IN, TUITIONFEE_OUT, DEBT_MDN, and MD_EARN_WNE_P10. Setting errors to coerce means that any value that cannot be interpreted as a number is replaced with NaN rather than raising an exception. This operation addressed the data type inconsistency created by the presence of suppressed values and any other non-numeric residuals that remained after the replacement step. After this conversion, all four columns contained only float values or NaN, making them suitable for arithmetic operations, summary statistics, and plotting. Without this conversion, attempts to compute means, medians, or correlations on these columns would either fail or produce incorrect results.

The third operation was standardizing the UNITID column to string type in all three datasets before performing any merges. This was done by applying the astype(str) method to the UNITID column in each dataframe immediately after loading. As described in the Data Quality section, mismatched UNITID types between datasets caused silent merge failures that were difficult to diagnose. Converting all three to string type before joining guaranteed that the join key would be treated consistently regardless of how each source originally encoded the identifier. This is a simple but essential operation that ensures the integrity of the integration step.

The fourth operation was column selection. Rather than carrying all columns from each dataset through the pipeline, we explicitly defined lists of columns to retain from each source and selected only those before merging. This reduced the width of the merged dataset significantly from several thousand columns to a manageable set of variables, making the file easier to inspect, faster to load in subsequent steps, and less prone to confusion about which variables came from which source. The column lists are defined at the top of clean.py so they are transparent and easy to modify if the analysis scope changes.

The fifth operation was row filtering after merging. After producing the integrated dataset, rows were dropped if they were missing values in any of the three core financial variables: TUITIONFEE_IN, DEBT_MDN, and MD_EARN_WNE_P10. This filtering step ensures that only institutions with complete financial profiles across all three key dimensions are included in the analysis, reducing the risk of misleading visualizations or summary statistics caused by institutions with partial data. We considered multiple alternatives before settling on this approach. Mean or median imputation was considered but rejected because the missing data in this context is not random. Values are suppressed specifically when student populations are small, which means institutions with missing values may be systematically different from those that do report, and substituting aggregate statistics would obscure rather than address that difference. We also considered including institutions with partial data and computing statistics only over available values, but this would have made comparisons across institutions less reliable. Dropping incomplete rows was the most conservative and transparent approach.

## Findings

Earnings by institution type: The bar chart in results/figures/earnings_by_control.png shows median earnings ten years post-enrollment broken down by institution control type: public, private nonprofit, and private for-profit. Private nonprofit institutions have the highest median earnings among the three groups, followed by public institutions, with private for-profit institutions showing the lowest median earnings. The difference between private nonprofit and for-profit institutions is substantial and represents a meaningful gap in graduate financial outcomes. This finding suggests that institution type is a strong predictor of graduate earnings even without accounting for other factors such as selectivity, field of study, or geographic location. One interpretation is that private nonprofit institutions tend to produce graduates who enter higher-paying industries and occupations at greater rates, potentially due to stronger alumni networks, career services, or program prestige. Another interpretation is that students who enroll at private nonprofits may have had stronger pre-existing academic or socioeconomic advantages that predict higher earnings regardless of where they enrolled. The data alone cannot distinguish between these explanations, but the pattern is clear and consistent.

Student debt versus earnings: The scatter plot in results/figures/debt_vs_earnings.png shows median student debt on the horizontal axis and median earnings on the vertical axis, with each point representing one institution and points colored by control type. A diagonal reference line is drawn to mark where annual earnings equal total cumulative debt. Institutions above this line have graduates who earn more per year than their total debt at the ten-year mark, while institutions below the line have graduates whose annual earnings do not exceed their debt. The majority of institutions across all three control types fall above the reference line, indicating that graduates at most colleges and universities are in a financially viable position relative to their debt by ten years post-enrollment. However, a notable cluster of private for-profit institutions falls at or below the line, meaning that graduates from those schools carry debt that is comparable to or exceeds their annual earnings a decade after starting school. This pattern raises serious questions about the financial value provided by those institutions relative to the debt burden they impose on students. Public and private nonprofit institutions cluster more favorably above the line, with private nonprofits showing the widest spread in both debt and earnings, reflecting the diversity of institutions in that category from small liberal arts colleges to large research universities.

Tuition distribution by institution level: The box plot in results/figures/tuition_distribution.png shows the distribution of in-state tuition and fees across institution levels: 4-year, 2-year, and less-than-2-year. Four-year institutions have dramatically higher median tuition and a much wider interquartile range than two-year institutions, reflecting the greater diversity in cost among bachelor's degree granting schools, which range from low-cost regional state universities to high-cost private research universities. Two-year institutions, which are predominantly community colleges, are clustered at much lower price points with much less variation, consistent with their mission of providing affordable access to postsecondary education. Less-than-two-year institutions, which are typically certificate programs, show the lowest tuition overall but also correspond to the weakest earnings outcomes in other analyses, suggesting that lower cost alone does not guarantee financial value when graduate earnings are factored in.

Federal aid and earnings: The bar chart in results/figures/aid_vs_earnings.png compares median earnings at institutions where federal loans are distributed to students versus those where no federal loan activity is recorded. Institutions with federal aid availability show modestly higher median earnings on average. This pattern likely reflects structural differences between the two groups rather than a direct effect of aid availability on earnings. Institutions without federal loan activity tend to be small, non-traditional, or newly established programs that also tend to show weaker earnings outcomes, while institutions with active federal aid programs are more likely to be established colleges and universities with stronger labor market connections for their graduates.

The top ten institutions by median earnings ten years post-enrollment are saved at results/tables/top10_earnings.csv. These institutions are dominated by highly selective private nonprofit universities and specialized professional schools, particularly in fields such as medicine, engineering, and business. The bottom ten institutions by median earnings are saved at results/tables/bottom10_earnings.csv and are predominantly for-profit certificate and short-term associate degree programs. Summary statistics for all key variables including count, mean, median, standard deviation, minimum, and quartiles are saved at results/tables/summary_statistics.csv and provide a complete distributional picture of the analysis dataset.

## Future Work

One of the most significant lessons learned from this project is how much of the analytical work involves data preparation rather than analysis itself. The majority of time was spent handling suppressed values, resolving type mismatches, standardizing identifiers, and debugging merge operations that produced incorrect results silently. This experience reinforced the importance of systematic data profiling before beginning any analysis and of building cleaning steps that are explicit, documented, and automated rather than applied interactively and forgotten. It also reinforced how deceptive data preparation problems can be, since many of the issues we encountered did not produce error messages but instead produced quietly incorrect results that required careful inspection of intermediate outputs to detect. Future projects would benefit from building validation checks directly into each pipeline stage rather than relying on manual inspection.

One direction for future work would be a longitudinal analysis tracking how tuition-to-earnings ratios have changed over time for the same institutions. The College Scorecard releases updated cohort files annually, and historical versions are also available for download. This means it would be possible to construct a panel dataset spanning a decade or more and examine whether the financial return on a degree at a given institution has been improving, declining, or remaining stable over time. The current analysis uses a single snapshot and cannot distinguish between institutions that have always had strong outcomes and those that have recently improved or declined. Understanding these trends would be considerably more informative for prospective students making enrollment decisions today than a static comparison.

A second direction would be incorporating field of study data. The College Scorecard also provides earnings data broken down by field of study at each institution, not just at the overall institution level. Incorporating this dimension would allow for much more precise comparisons, such as the earnings outcomes for computer science graduates at public universities versus private nonprofit universities, or the debt-to-earnings ratio for humanities graduates at high-tuition versus low-tuition schools. This level of specificity would make the analysis considerably more actionable for students who are deciding both where to enroll and what to study, and would help disentangle the effect of institution type from the effect of field of study choice on earnings outcomes.

A third direction would be using net price instead of published tuition figures. Published tuition and fees represent the sticker price that institutions charge before any grants or scholarships are applied. Most students, particularly those with financial need, pay less than this amount due to institutional and federal aid. Net price after grants and scholarships gives a more accurate picture of what students actually pay out of pocket or need to borrow. The IPEDS Financial Aid dataset contains net price broken down by student income category, which would allow for analysis of affordability across different socioeconomic groups rather than treating all students at an institution as facing the same cost. A net-price based analysis would be more equitable and more reflective of the financial reality faced by students from different backgrounds.

A fourth direction would be more carefully addressing selection effects in the analysis. The correlations identified in this project between institution type and earnings outcomes do not establish causation. Students who attend selective private nonprofit institutions may have had stronger earnings prospects regardless of where they enrolled, due to pre-existing advantages in academic preparation, family wealth, or social networks. Future work could attempt to address this by incorporating institutional selectivity measures such as acceptance rates or standardized test score ranges as control variables, or by using quasi-experimental methods such as regression discontinuity designs that exploit thresholds in admissions decisions to compare students who are similar in measured characteristics but attended different types of institutions.

A fifth direction would be adding geographic analysis to the findings. The current analysis aggregates institutions nationally and does not explore whether the relationship between tuition and earnings varies by region, state, or labor market. Adding state-level visualizations such as choropleth maps or regional comparisons would help reveal geographic patterns in the financial return on higher education and could be particularly useful for students who plan to remain in their home state or region after graduation, where local labor market conditions and state tuition policies interact to shape the actual financial return on their degree.

## Challenges

The most significant technical challenge encountered in this project was the "PrivacySuppressed" placeholder in the College Scorecard dataset. Because the suppressed values appeared inside columns that would otherwise contain numeric data such as tuition, debt, and earnings, pandas read those entire columns as object type when the file was first loaded. This meant that attempting to compute means, medians, or any other numeric summary statistics on those columns before addressing the suppressed values would either throw a TypeError at runtime or return unexpected results with no useful diagnostic message. The challenge was not just identifying and fixing the issue, but first diagnosing why operations that appeared straightforward were not working correctly. Diagnosing it required carefully inspecting column dtypes after loading, running value_counts on individual columns to identify unexpected string values, and tracing through the pandas documentation to understand how mixed-type columns behave when numeric operations are applied to them. Once the issue was identified, the fix was simple, but the diagnosis process was time-consuming and required methodical investigation.

A second major challenge was the UNITID type mismatch across datasets. When we first attempted to merge the College Scorecard with the IPEDS files, the resulting dataframe appeared to complete successfully but had far fewer rows than expected and most of the columns from the IPEDS files were filled entirely with NaN. There was no error message or warning from pandas indicating that anything had gone wrong. The merge simply produced no matches because integer and string representations of the same institution ID are not considered equal in a pandas merge operation. Discovering the root cause required systematically checking the dtype of the UNITID column in each source file separately, printing sample values side by side to compare them visually, and realizing that the values were encoded differently despite representing the same identifier. This experience highlighted the importance of explicitly validating intermediate merge outputs rather than assuming that a merge completed correctly just because it did not raise an exception.

A third challenge was managing the size and width of the College Scorecard CSV file. The raw file contains several thousand columns covering every variable collected across all Scorecard surveys, and it covers thousands of institutions. Loading the full file into pandas during development caused significant slowdowns on local machines and in some cases led to memory errors that crashed the Python process. Resolving this required switching to the usecols parameter in pd.read_csv to load only the specific columns needed for our analysis rather than the entire file. This approach reduced memory usage dramatically but required knowing the exact column names in advance, which meant carefully reviewing the Scorecard data documentation before writing any code. It also made the loading code more brittle to changes in column naming across different Scorecard releases.

A fourth challenge was the temporal misalignment across the three datasets. The College Scorecard, IPEDS Characteristics, and IPEDS Financial Aid files each come from different survey years or cohort definitions. While UNITID ensures that institutions are correctly matched, the variables from different years may not perfectly reflect the same point in time. For example, tuition figures reported in the Scorecard may correspond to a different academic year than the financial aid figures from the SFA2223 survey. We documented this limitation and accepted it as an inherent constraint of working with administrative data from multiple sources that are collected on different cycles. Fully resolving it would require identifying and downloading versions of each dataset from the same academic year, which would have complicated the acquisition process significantly.

A fifth challenge was deciding how to handle missing data after merging. A substantial proportion of institutions had missing values in one or more financial variables even after removing suppressed values and performing type conversions. Deciding whether to drop, impute, or retain those rows required careful thought about why the values were missing and how each choice would affect the validity of the analysis. Imputation was considered but rejected because the missing data mechanism in this dataset is not random. Values tend to be missing specifically for small institutions or small student subgroups, which means those institutions may have systematically different characteristics than those that do report. Substituting imputed values would obscure this distinction and potentially introduce bias into the analysis results. We settled on the conservative approach of dropping rows missing the core financial variables and documenting this choice clearly.

## Reproducing

To reproduce the complete workflow from data acquisition through analysis results, follow the steps below. The workflow has been tested on macOS and Linux environments with Python 3.9 and above.

First, ensure that Python 3.9 or higher is installed on your machine along with pip. Snakemake can be installed via pip as described below. Clone the repository and navigate into it:

    git clone https://github.com/alishaa5/IS477-SP26.git
    cd IS477-SP26

Second, install all required Python dependencies using the requirements.txt file included in the repository:

    pip install -r requirements.txt

This will install pandas, numpy, matplotlib, requests, and snakemake along with their dependencies. If you are using a virtual environment, activate it before running this command.

Third, run the full automated workflow using Snakemake with a single command:

    snakemake --cores 1

Snakemake will automatically execute the scripts in the correct order: acquire.py first to download and extract the raw datasets, then clean.py to handle missing values and type conversions, then merge.py to integrate the three datasets into a single file, and finally analyze.py to generate all figures and summary tables. All intermediate and final outputs are written to the data/ and results/ directories. Snakemake will skip any steps whose outputs are already present and up to date, so if you only want to rerun a specific step you can delete its output files and run the workflow again.

If you prefer to run the scripts manually without Snakemake, execute them in the following order:

    python acquire.py
    python clean.py
    python merge.py
    python analyze.py

Note that acquire.py downloads data directly from the College Scorecard server and the NCES server, so an active internet connection is required for this step. The script prints the SHA-256 checksum of each downloaded file to the console so that file integrity can be verified against the values recorded in this report. If the raw data files are already present in data/raw/, acquire.py will skip the download and print the existing checksums instead.

Expected outputs after running the full pipeline include the raw ZIP and extracted CSV files in data/raw/, the cleaned individual dataset files, the integrated dataset at data/merged/merged_dataset.csv, four figures saved as PNG files in results/figures/ (earnings_by_control.png, debt_vs_earnings.png, tuition_distribution.png, and aid_vs_earnings.png), and three tables saved as CSV files in results/tables/ (summary_statistics.csv, top10_earnings.csv, and bottom10_earnings.csv).
